# The Core Business Question

_The recurring question Maor surfaced (08/07 conversation) — the one the workshop is built around._

## The surface question (how it lands on the analyst's desk)
The business/stakeholder does a drill-down and comes back with a reordering claim:

> _"Overall our chart is ranked A > B > C by EPC. But when I look **specifically at Bing**, partner B actually has the highest EPC — $9 vs partner A's $8. So let's reorder the chart for Bing / promote B."_

It arrives as an apparent difference **inside a slice** (a channel, a platform, a segment, a recent week), with an action already implied.

## The question as Maor actually defines it
The analyst's real job is **not** to confirm the reordering — it's to decide whether that apparent difference is **a real, actionable signal or an artifact.** In his words, roughly:

> _"The fact that you saw $9 EPC here and $8 there — that's not necessarily real. It could be very random. It could be that you looked at a segment with barely any data. It could be some seasonality-specific effect. It could be that what you saw in Bing last week just isn't representative — and now this isn't necessarily the right fix."_

So the operative question is:

> **Given an apparent difference between two options in a drill-down slice, is there a statistically defensible case that it's real — and big enough to move revenue by a point or two — or is it net an artifact of thin data, seasonality, or noise?**

## The alternative explanations Maor named (what "artifact" means, concretely)
The difference could be explained away by any of:
- **Small sample in the slice** — you drilled into a cut with little data.
- **Seasonality / timing** — a day-of-week or period-specific effect, not a stable one.
- **Non-representativeness / recency** — "last week in Bing" isn't next week; the ordering isn't stable.
- **Plain randomness** — noise around a small ($9 vs $8) gap.

## The decision and the stakes
The action on the table is concrete and reversible-with-cost: **reorder the chart / change a partner's position for that segment.** The analyst has to hand the business a defensible statement — _"there's a real case here, act"_ vs _"this is within noise; acting on it is closer to flipping a coin"_ — and be honest when it's the latter.

## Why it's *the* recurring question
The same shape recurs no matter the nouns: **Bing vs Google, partner B vs partner A in a segment, CTR yesterday vs last week.** It's always _"an apparent difference in a slice — is it real enough to act on?"_ That's the question the deterministic analysis has to answer the same way every time.
